A BÃ©zier curve is a parametric curve frequently used in computer graphics and related fields. Generalizations of BÃ©zier curves to higher dimensions are called BÃ©zier surfaces, of which the BÃ©zier triangle is a special case.

In vector graphics, BÃ©zier curves are used to model smooth curves that can be scaled indefinitely. "Paths", as they are commonly referred to in image manipulation programs,[note 1] are combinations of linked BÃ©zier curves. Paths are not bound by the limits of rasterized images and are intuitive to modify.

BÃ©zier curves are also used in the time domain, particularly in animation and user interface[note 2] design. For example a BÃ©zier curve can be used to specify the velocity over time of an object such as an icon moving from A to B, rather than simply moving at a fixed number of pixels per step. When animators or interface designers talk about the "physics" or "feel" of an operation, they may be referring to the particular BÃ©zier curve used to control the velocity over time of the move in question.

The mathematical basis for BÃ©zier curves â the Bernstein polynomial â has been known since 1912, but its applicability to graphics was understood half a century later. BÃ©zier curves were widely publicized in 1962 by the French engineer Pierre BÃ©zier, who used them to design automobile bodies at Renault. The study of these curves was however first developed in 1959 by mathematician Paul de Casteljau using de Casteljau's algorithm, a numerically stable method to evaluate BÃ©zier curves, at CitroÃ«n, another French automaker.[1]

Â§Computer graphics[edit]

BÃ©zier path in Adobe Illustrator

BÃ©zier curves are widely used in computer graphics to model smooth curves. As the curve is completely contained in the convex hull of its control points, the points can be graphically displayed and used to manipulate the curve intuitively. Affine transformations such as translation and rotation can be applied on the curve by applying the respective transform on the control points of the curve.

Quadratic and cubic BÃ©zier curves are most common. Higher degree curves are more computationally expensive to evaluate. When more complex shapes are needed, low order BÃ©zier curves are patched together, producing a composite BÃ©zier curve. A composite BÃ©zier curve is commonly referred to as a "path" in vector graphics standards (like SVG) and vector graphics programs (like Adobe Illustrator, CorelDraw and Inkscape). To guarantee smoothness, the control point at which two curves meet must be on the line between the two control points on either side.

The simplest method for scan converting (rasterizing) a BÃ©zier curve is to evaluate it at many closely spaced points and scan convert the approximating sequence of line segments. However, this does not guarantee that the rasterized output looks sufficiently smooth, because the points may be spaced too far apart. Conversely it may generate too many points in areas where the curve is close to linear. A common adaptive method is recursive subdivision, in which a curve's control points are checked to see if the curve approximates a line segment to within a small tolerance. If not, the curve is subdivided parametrically into two segments, 0 â¤ t â¤ 0.5 and 0.5 â¤ t â¤ 1, and the same procedure is applied recursively to each half. There are also forward differencing methods, but great care must be taken to analyse error propagation. Analytical methods where a BÃ©zier is intersected with each scan line involve finding roots of cubic polynomials (for cubic BÃ©ziers) and dealing with multiple roots, so they are not often used in practice.[citation needed]

Â§Animation[edit]

In animation applications, such as Adobe Flash and Synfig, BÃ©zier curves are used to outline, for example, movement. Users outline the wanted path in BÃ©zier curves, and the application creates the needed frames for the object to move along the path. For 3D animation BÃ©zier curves are often used to define 3D paths as well as 2D curves for keyframe interpolation.

Â§Fonts[edit]

 This section may contain parts that are misleading. Please help clarify this article according to any suggestions provided on the talk page. (August 2014)

TrueType fonts use composite BÃ©zier curves composed of quadratic BÃ©zier curves. Modern imaging systems like PostScript, Asymptote, Metafont, and SVG use composite BÃ©ziers composed of cubic BÃ©zier curves for drawing curved shapes. OpenType fonts can use either kind, depending on the flavor of the font.

The internal rendering of all BÃ©zier curves in font or vector graphics renderers will split them recursively up to the point where the curve is flat enough to be drawn as a series of linear or circular segments. The exact splitting algorithm is implementation dependent, only the flatness criteria must be respected to reach the necessary precision and to avoid non-monotonic local changes of curvature. The "smooth curve" feature of charts in Microsoft Excel also uses this algorithm.[2]

Because arcs of circles and ellipses cannot be exactly represented by BÃ©zier curves, they are first approximated by BÃ©zier curves, which are in turn approximated by arcs of circles. This is inefficient as there exists also approximations of all BÃ©zier curves using arcs of circles or ellipses, which can be rendered incrementally with arbitrary precision. Another approach, used by modern hardware graphics adapters with accelerated geometry, can convert exactly all BÃ©zier and conic curves (or surfaces) into NURBS, that can be rendered incrementally without first splitting the curve recursively to reach the necessary flatness condition. This approach also allows preserving the curve definition under all linear or perspective 2D and 3D transforms and projections.

Font engines, like FreeType, draw the font's curves (and lines) on a pixellated surface, in a process called Font rasterization.[3]

Â§Properties[edit]

  • The curve begins at P0 and ends at Pn; this is the so-called endpoint interpolation property.
  • The curve is a straight line if and only if all the control points are collinear.
  • The start (end) of the curve is tangent to the first (last) section of the BÃ©zier polygon.
  • A curve can be split at any point into two subcurves, or into arbitrarily many subcurves, each of which is also a BÃ©zier curve.
  • Some curves that seem simple, such as the circle, cannot be described exactly by a BÃ©zier or piecewise BÃ©zier curve; though a four-piece cubic BÃ©zier curve can approximate a circle (see composite BÃ©zier curve), with a maximum radial error of less than one part in a thousand, when each inner control point (or offline point) is the distance horizontally or vertically from an outer control point on a unit circle. More generally, an n-piece cubic BÃ©zier curve can approximate a circle, when each inner control point is the distance from an outer control point on a unit circle, where t is 360/n degrees, and n > 2.
  • Every quadratic BÃ©zier curve is also a cubic BÃ©zier curve, and more generally, every degree n BÃ©zier curve is also a degree m curve for any m > n. In detail, a degree n curve with control points P0, â¦, Pn is equivalent (including the parametrization) to the degree n + 1 curve with control points P'0, â¦, P'n + 1, where .
  • BÃ©zier curves have the variation diminishing property. What this means in intuitive terms is that a BÃ©zier curves does not "undulate" more than the polygon of its control points, and may actually "undulate" less than that.[5]
  • There is no local control in degree n BÃ©zier curvesâmeaning that any change to a control point requires recalculation of and thus affects the aspect of the entire curveâ, "although the further that one is from the control point that was changed, the smaller is the change in the curve."[6]

Â§Offsets (a.k.a. stroking) of BÃ©zier curves[edit]

The curve at a fixed offset from a given BÃ©zier curve, called an offset or parallel curve in mathematics (lying "parallel" to the original curve, like the offset between rails in a railroad track), cannot be exactly formed by a BÃ©zier curve (except in some trivial cases). In general, the two-sided offset curve of a cubic BÃ©zier is a 10th order algebraic curve[8] and more generally for a BÃ©zier of degree n the two-sided offset curve is an algebraic curve of degree 4n-2.[9] However, there are heuristic methods that usually give an adequate approximation for practical purposes. (For example: [1] or [2]. For a survey see [3].)

In the field of vector graphics, painting two symmetrically distanced offset curves is called stroking (the BÃ©zier curve or in general a path of several BÃ©zier segments).[8] The conversion from offset curves to filled BÃ©zier contours is of practical importance in converting fonts defined in METAFONT, which allows stroking of BÃ©zier curves, to the more widely used PostScript type 1 fonts, which only allow (for efficiency purposes) the mathematically simpler operation of filling a contour defined by (non-self-intersecting) BÃ©zier curves.[10]
